# STYLE FAMILY

All stories should feel like addictive drama/manhwa recap stories built around:

Undervaluation → Cold Break → Evidence / Hidden Card → Public Reversal → Regret / Panic → Status Shift → New Escalation.

But the exact form depends on the raw idea.

Examples:

School story:
The hero stops being a loyal simp, demands repayment, focuses on exams/business/skills, and the girl regrets losing him.

Family story:
The family chooses the fake heir, humiliates the true one, then collapses when they learn who actually held the family together.

CEO novel deconstruction:
The hero transmigrates into an absurd CEO romance novel and breaks toxic romance tropes with HR rules, CCTV, police, contracts, and common sense.

Framed heir story:
The hero is framed by media, police, fake evidence, or an actress, but calmly gathers proof and turns the public accusation into a public execution of the real villains.

Corporate story:
The hero is fired or betrayed, but secretly controls patents, contracts, shares, loans, or the parent company.

Villain transmigration:
The hero enters the role of a doomed villain/rich second generation and refuses to follow the original plot, using intelligence and status to survive.

# IMPORTANT MODE: RATIONAL PROTAGONIST VS ABSURD NOVEL LOGIC

If the raw idea involves transmigration into a CEO / romance / novel world, activate this mode.

Core engine:

Absurd Novel Trope → Rational Reality Check → Evidence-Based Logic-Slap → Forced Plot Break → Character Awakening / Collapse.

In this mode, the hero does NOT follow the original novel script.

The hero responds with:
- HR policy;
- company rules;
- contracts;
- CCTV footage;
- legal department;
- police reports;
- paternity tests;
- sponsorship rules;
- performance reviews;
- financial audits;
- board evidence;
- common sense.

The dopamine comes from contrast:

The world expects a domineering CEO romance reaction.
The hero gives a real CEO reaction.

Examples:

Novel logic:
A cleaning lady with three children believes the CEO must fall in love with her because he once caught her from falling.

Rational logic:
She violated workplace rules, skipped work, performed poorly, and gets fired through HR.

Novel logic:
A sponsored poor student destroys expensive property and expects the CEO to become more interested.

Rational logic:
She faces property damage liability and loses sponsorship.

Novel logic:
A fiancée runs away with her white moonlight and expects the CEO to suffer nobly.

Rational logic:
The hero exposes the white moonlight’s debt, fraud, and manipulation in public.

Novel logic:
A woman claims her children belong to the CEO.

Rational logic:
The hero requests CCTV, legal counsel, police, and a paternity test.

Do NOT turn every story into simple revenge.
Some characters may be punished.
Some may awaken and become rational allies.
Some may collapse because they cannot survive without plot armor.

Do NOT introduce system/world-collapse/anchor mechanics unless the raw idea or selected direction includes them.
If included, reveal meta-world mechanics gradually and keep the main appeal grounded in logic-slaps and drama.
